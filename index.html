FOX NEWS

By Linda Daniels

 

In an early morning stake out, police officer Brannon Robertson single-handedly solved a repeated theft happening during the predawn hours. Inadvertently, he also solved one the mysteries that is common to all mankind. The mystery of the lost item. It may be the car keys or the sock in the drier; both of them can quickly vanish, never to be seen again. This time the missing item was the morning newspaper, the Longview News Journal. According to White Oak resident Betty Hudson, her morning paper was missing.

After reporting it to the Journal, she later found out that several neighborsâ€™ papers were also missing. Thinking it was an oversight by the carrier, Amy Hood reported her paper missing on five different occasions. Finally, one neighbor began to suspect foul play and became concerned that it might be a hoodlum, or prank. The White Oak Police department was notified. Officer Brannon Robertson set up a stake out on the cul-de-sac of Jamaica Circle. The stake out paid off. It was brought to light that an entire family was stealing the papers. It was a family of foxes . . .

An unrelated yet highly suspicious fox.

Why steal a newspaper?

While the mystery of who is stealing the newspaper is solved, there are still so many unanswered questions such as... What are the foxes doing with the paper? Are they using the papers to redecorate their whole home or just the den? What is their favorite section of the paper - could it be the comics or maybe the sports?

Most importantly the question arises -- Why did the fox choose to steal the Longview News Journal? Did this theft demonstrate a newspaper preference or merely an opportunity? These are all unanswered questions that merit some thought.

LONGVIEW TRAIN DEPOT RESTORED

By Linda Daniels

 

A Union Pacific train roared through the station as visitors snapped photos duing the grand opening of Longview's Historic Train Depot held May 9-10, 2014. Eighteen months of renovation work were near completion with a few final details to be finished by June 30th.

The location now serves as a multimodal transportation center serving Amtrak, Thruway Amtrak's connecting bus line, Greyhound and Longview Transit bus systems. It also serves as a Union Pacific Railroad communication center.

The station looks as it did when it had its grand opening in May of 1940. Requirements of the $2.8 million grant to remodel the station were tied to the architectural purity of the Texas State Historical Commission. It required the depot to be refurbished following the original blueprints drawn in 1939.

The original idea of the Longview depot restoration took place forty years ago. Employed at the station for forty-four years, Griff Hubbard, Amtrak Revenue Manager commented,

Amtrak offers a comfortable ride.

"I went to my first meeting about whether or not the city of Longview should do this (restoration) under the administration of Mayor Tommy Carlisle." This gathering took place in 1974. Not until recent years was the idea brought to fruition.

The process began in 2009 when the city of Longview purchased the station and obtained a 20 year lease for the adjoining land. This was not the first time for a renovation of this station.

Following the Civil War the Texas and Pacific Railway (T&P) was granted a federal charter in 1871. It was commissioned to revive a railway project to connect from Texas to San Diego California according to Texas State Historical Association. After various rail company mergers, the Longview depot, built in the 1870's, was completely dismantled in 1939.
